Course Abstract

Name of the course: The Visual Icon--Introduction to Aesthetics

Name of the Discipline & the Programme: IDDS (SLA)

Usual time of occurrence (Semester – Sequence): Jun-Oct Semester for PGDPD 1st sem & 3 rd sem students

Duration: 1 WU

Course category: Theory

Credits: 1

Prerequisites: The students are expected to have a spirit of inquiry and a thirst for exploration. Reading and comprehension of English are important prerequisites.

Relevance

This course explores the subject of Aesthetics in both the Indian and Western traditions, focusing particularly on the Indian tradition. In exploring the subject of aesthetics in the Indian and Western traditions, it has art as its central axis.

Objective

The broad objectives of the course are:

  • To provide students with theoretical tools of understanding art and culture
  • To give a brief survey of aesthetic theories and principles by which any product is judged in a given epistemic field.
  • To examine and explore the link between art, design and aesthetics
  • To engage the students to think in abstract terms, to convert abstract perceptions to concrete representations,
  • To try and articulate (in speech and writing) their perception of the aesthetic.

Course Contents

The fulcrum of the course is art, beauty and philosophy . The module begins by raising questions such as what is art? What is design? What is beauty? What is good art ? what is not? The notion of value in art and so on. In the Western tradition, we cover concepts like ‘realism’ (Plato), ‘empiricism’ (Aristotle) and ‘sublimity’ (Longinus), and undertake a brief journey through various other theories leading to the post modern age. The questions of power and politics play a crucial role in the study of art and culture and the module will seek to familiarise students with such theories. We also look at theories of aesthetic perception in Classical India. For this, we will take recourse to the Rasa theory as espoused by Bharatamuni (Natyasastra). In the process, we try to explore the concept of bhavas, the process of generation of rasa, the essence of dramaturgy and poesie, etc.

Day 1: Basic discussion on the term, “aesthetics”; The notion of beauty; The term aesthetics—in India, different senses in the West; Aesthetics and arts; aesthetics and philosophy—the aesthetic paradox; Central questions of aesthetics: on art, the aesthetic object, the notion of good art, the usefulness of art, art and morality etc.

Day 2: Discussion on Western philosophy and aesthetics

Day 3: Discussion on modernist and post modernist aesthetics

Day 4: Discussion on India; Rasa-bhava theory and Natyashastra

Day 5: Discussion on Indian aesthetics continues through examples

List of Assignments

The assignments in this course provide the platform to understand the concepts in the perspective of different disciplines and their application potential. They also enable us to see the gaps between theory and practice as it applies to design. The assignments designed for the course will demand skills in understanding of abstract concepts and articulation of the same in visualisation through one’s senses and skills, speech and writing abilities. Please note that the assignments aim at gauging understanding of the issues taken up in the course as a whole rather than creative abilities. One end of the course written assignment in about 1500-2000 words that could be a reflection on any one of the topics covered in the course.

Methodology

Classroom will be treated as a site where the learning process is deeply embedded in a horizontal dialogic process. After three days of classroom discussion, the students are expected to interact with people outside the classroom on issues that they chose for their presentation.

The course would follow the following modes:

  • Perspective lectures
  • Group discussions and debates
  • Library work / readings
  • Class work: discussions, presentations
  • Screenings of films
  • Field Visit

Evaluation Criteria

Evaluation shall be done on the following criteria: Comprehension and conceptual clarity; reflective thinking; communication; attendance and time management and participation.
